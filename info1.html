Christ the Redeemer

An Icon of Brazil & Peace

1 The Early Concept (1850s – 1880s)

The vision began nearly 70 years before construction. A priest named Pedro Maria Boss suggested a monument to honor Princess Isabel. She requested it be dedicated to "Christ the Redeemer" instead. After Brazil became a republic in 1889, the project was paused for decades.

2 A Religious Revival (1920 – 1922)

Post-WWI, the Catholic Circle of Rio revived the idea to reclaim the nation's spiritual identity. During "Monument Week," the Brazilian people donated $250,000—making the statue entirely funded by private faith-based contributions.

3 Designing the Icon

The original "Christ with a ball" design was simplified into an Art Deco masterpiece by Heitor da Silva Costa and Carlos Oswald. The final design features Christ himself as a cross, symbolizing a blessing of peace over the city.

4 International Collaboration

While Brazil led the project, French-Polish sculptor Paul Landowski created the head and hands in Paris. Romanian sculptor Gheorghe Leonida carved the serenity of Christ’s face, creating a global artistic fusion.

5 The Construction Phase (1922 – 1931)

Built with reinforced concrete, the statue is covered in 6 million soapstone tiles. These tiles give the monument a soft glow and protect it from extreme weather. Local women spent months gluing these tiles onto sheets for application.

6 The Grand Inauguration

On October 12, 1931, the lights were triggered via radio signal. Today, it stands 98 feet tall as one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, surviving 3 to 6 lightning strikes annually.
