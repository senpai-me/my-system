Taj Mahal

The Crown of Palaces & Eternal Love

1 The Origin of Love (1631)

Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan commissioned the Taj Mahal in memory of his favorite wife, Mumtaz Mahal, who died during childbirth. Devastated by her death, Shah Jahan vowed to build a tomb so beautiful that the world would never forget their love.

2 Architect & Construction

Led by chief architect Ustad Ahmad Lahori, the project took approximately 22 years (1632–1653) to complete. Over 20,000 artisans and workers from India, Persia, and the Ottoman Empire were brought to Agra to create this masterpiece.

3 Materials & Logistics

The white marble was brought from Makrana, Rajasthan, nearly 400km away. More than 1,000 elephants were used to transport construction materials. 28 types of precious and semi-precious stones, like Lapis Lazuli and Jade, were inlaid into the marble.

4 Optical Illusions & Symmetry

The Taj Mahal is famous for its perfect symmetry. However, to prevent the four minarets from falling on the main dome during an earthquake, they were built leaning slightly outward. Also, as you move closer to the gate, the Taj seems to get smaller—a deliberate optical illusion.

5 The Charbagh Garden

The tomb is set in a 300-meter square "Charbagh" (Mughal garden). The garden uses raised pathways that divide each of the four quarters of the garden into 16 sunken flowerbeds, representing the paradise described in ancient texts.

6 UNESCO & Legacy

In 1983, the Taj Mahal became a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Today, it attracts 7 to 8 million visitors annually and stands as one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, symbolizing India's rich history and architectural brilliance.
